As a scholar of Comparative Politics and American Politics, my primary area of specialization is democratic studies, with a particular focus on the topics at the intersection of historical processes of (de)democratization and racial inclusion/exclusion. I explore these issues through a comparative and historical lens, with a regional emphasis on the United States and Western Europe. I am also interested in developing qualitative methods of political science, particularly discourse analysis, case study research, and Comparative Historical Analysis.

I completed my Ph.D. in Political Science at the University of Massachusetts Amherst under the guidance of Amel Ahmed, Jesse Rhodes, and Erik Bleich. My dissertation, titled Regime Contention and Racial Enfranchisement in the United States: From the Progressive Era to the Voting Rights Act of 1965, explores why there was no reform of racial enfranchisement—suffrage extension to racial minorities—during the Progressive Era despite several conducive factors, and why such a reform eventually occurred with the passage of the Voting Rights Act of 1965. I have presented my research at the annual conferences of NPSA, NYSPSA, ISA, MPSA, and APSA, and currently have a co-authored piece under review. I have also taught undergraduate courses on Race and American Politics and Introduction to Comparative Politics.

One of the most important decisions in life is choosing a career path and I have experienced a special journey before making this decision. My elder sister and I are both visually impaired individuals. She was a very industrious person who graduated from the most prestigious law school in Turkey as valedictorian. Contrary to her initial expectations, however, all of her applications for important positions both in bureaucracy and private sector were rejected due to her disability. The only available job opportunities were those that assume no genuine responsibility. It was my first cold confrontation with this institutionalized bias against physically impaired individuals. This incident occurred in the earlier days of my high school education and I felt that I was in the verge of deciding which way to go for the rest of my life: Whether I was to acquiescence to the confinements imposed on me and contend with an isolated life style, or I was to transcend the boundaries and strive hard to become a success story.

I chose the second path and have since dedicated myself to two primary goals. First, I sought a career where I could make a meaningful impact as an independent individual. Second, and perhaps more importantly, I aimed to challenge the common prejudices surrounding my disability. Academia seemed like the ideal avenue to achieve both goals for two key reasons—though, unfortunately, I now have serious doubts about these assumptions. First, I believed that success in academia was based almost entirely on one's intellectual abilities, not physical limitations, in a field that values objectivity and creativity above all else. Second, academia’s channels of knowledge—through books, lectures, conferences, and media—are accessible to all who wish to engage with and contribute to them. These reasons convinced me that my future belonged in the academic world, where I could do what I do best and, ideally, be valued solely for my contributions.

After graduating from high school as valedictorian, I took the National University Entrance Exam in 2008 and ranked 23rd out of approximately 1.7 million students. This was the highest score ever achieved by a disabled student in the exam’s nearly fifty-year history. As a result, I was accepted into the Business Administration program at TOBB University of Economics and Technology (UET), one of the top schools for finance with a reputation for academic excellence. However, while the program was prestigious, it didn’t fully ignite my intellectual passions. I had always been deeply interested in both domestic and global politics, and fortunately, UET offered high-performing students the opportunity to pursue a double major. Choosing to add International Relations (IR) to my studies was a turning point in my life.

From my very first courses in the International Relations (IR) department, I was captivated by its diverse focus. I found particular interest in International Relations theory, international political economy (IPE), globalization, and the role of non-state actors. After a few semesters, I sought to combine my two academic tracks—applying my financial and statistical knowledge to the literature of IR—and develop as a student capable of conducting both qualitative and quantitative research. This diverse curriculum inspired me to pursue an interdisciplinary approach, focusing especially on IPE. Upon graduating with dual majors in Business Administration and International Relations from TOBB UET, with GPAs of 4.00/4.00 and 3.56/4.00 respectively, and as valedictorian, I began my MA in Political Science and International Relations at Koç University, home to one of Turkey’s few graduate programs with a specific emphasis on IPE. At Koc University, I had the privilege to work as a teaching and research assistant for Ziya Onis and Bahar Rumelili and I wrote my MA thesis on the rethinking of global hegemony in light of the recent rise of China under their mentorship along with Hakan Ovunc Ongur.

Although I began my Ph.D. at the University of Massachusetts Amherst in 2015 with a focus on international political economy (IPE) and international relations theory, my academic trajectory evolved significantly due to my exposure to a broader curriculum, a new culture, and diverse life experiences. Interacting with individuals from various backgrounds and life paths expanded my interpretive lens, reshaping how I understood both my personal story and scholarly identity. Previously, I saw my experience of marginalization as rooted solely in my physical differences, and I aimed to overcome this by excelling within the majority's meritocratic framework, viewing it as a story of personal triumph. However, my encounters with others who faced exclusion based on ascriptive identities illuminated broader social boundaries, even within so-called established democracies. This realization compelled me to critically examine the relationship between democratic governance and demographic diversity during my Ph.D. studies.
